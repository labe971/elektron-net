## The Right to Be Forgotten: From Legal Principle to Protocol Law

The right to be forgotten is not a radical idea. It is one of the oldest intuitions in human civilisation: that a person should not be defined forever by their past. What is new — and what Elektron Net encodes at the protocol level — is the recognition that in the digital age, this right cannot be left to the goodwill of administrators, the jurisdiction of courts, or the compliance of corporations. It must be mathematics.

Every existing legal framework for the right to be forgotten depends on three fragile pillars:

- **A request** — the individual must know their data exists, know where it is held, and actively petition for its deletion.
- **A controller** — there must be an identifiable organisation with the legal obligation and technical capacity to comply.
- **A jurisdiction** — the law must reach across borders, which as courts have repeatedly found, it often cannot.

Elektron Net eliminates all three dependencies. By using this software and participating in this chain, every user explicitly and irrevocably exercises their right to be forgotten — **without any prior request, without any petition, without any controller, and without any jurisdictional boundary.** The 137-day pruning cycle enforces this right automatically, by mathematical consensus, as an inherent property of the protocol itself.

> **By running Elektron Net software or transacting on the Elektron Net chain, users expressly consent to — and affirmatively invoke — the universal right to be forgotten with respect to their transaction history. No request is necessary. No authority can override it. The right is exercised by design, not by petition.**

## Global Laws & Regulations — Article by Article

The following is a structured overview of the world's major legal instruments that recognise the right to be forgotten, the right to erasure, or equivalent privacy protections. Elektron Net's 137-day pruning mechanism is compatible with, and in many cases exceeds the requirements of, all frameworks listed below.

---

### 🇪🇺 European Union — GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)
**Regulation (EU) 2016/679 — in force since 25 May 2018**

- **Article 17** — *Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")*
  The data subject has the right to obtain from the controller the erasure of personal data without undue delay where: the data is no longer necessary for its original purpose; consent has been withdrawn; the data has been unlawfully processed; or the data subject objects and there is no overriding legitimate interest.

- **Article 17(2)** — Where data has been made public, the controller must take reasonable technical steps to inform other controllers of the erasure request.

- **Article 7 & 8, EU Charter of Fundamental Rights** — Right to respect for private life and the right to protection of personal data as constitutional guarantees.

- **Article 25** — *Data protection by design and by default*
  Controllers must implement technical measures that ensure, by default, only the minimum necessary personal data is processed. Elektron Net's protocol architecture is the definitive implementation of this principle.

- **Recitals 65 & 66** — Contextual explanation of why erasure rights exist: individuals should be able to have their data erased when its retention is no longer justified.

**Key CJEU Cases:**
- *Google Spain SL v. AEPD (C-131/12, 2014)* — Established the right to delisting from search engines as a derivation of the erasure right.
- *Google LLC v. CNIL (C-507/17, 2019)* — Determined that the territorial scope of GDPR erasure obligations applies within the EU; global enforcement remains contested.

---

### 🇬🇧 United Kingdom — UK GDPR
**Data Protection Act 2018, incorporating retained EU law**

- **Article 17, UK GDPR** — Mirrors EU GDPR Article 17 post-Brexit. Individuals have the right to erasure of personal data; requests must be processed within one month.
- **Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974** — Establishes the principle that past convictions become "spent" after a defined period, a statutory precursor to the right to be forgotten in the criminal context.

---

### 🇧🇷 Brazil — LGPD (Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados Pessoais)
**Law No. 13,709/2018 — in force since 18 September 2020**

- **Article 17** — Establishes the fundamental rights of data subjects to ownership and control of their personal data.
- **Article 18, IV** — Right to anonymisation, blocking, or *erasure* of unnecessary, excessive, or non-compliant personal data.
- **Article 18, VI** — Right to erasure upon withdrawal of consent, with certain legal exceptions.
- **Articles 15 & 16** — Define the conditions under which data processing must be terminated and personal data permanently eliminated ("eliminação").
- **Article 5, XVI** — Defines "elimination" as the deletion of data or the set of data stored in a database, regardless of the procedure used.

Compliance deadline for erasure requests: **15 days** (stricter than the GDPR's 30-day limit).

---

### 🇨🇦 Canada — PIPEDA & CPPA
**Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (S.C. 2000, c. 5)**
**Consumer Privacy Protection Act (Bill C-27, in legislative process)**

- **PIPEDA, Principle 5 (Schedule 1)** — Personal data shall be retained only as long as necessary to fulfil the identified purpose.
- **PIPEDA, Principle 9** — Individuals have the right to access personal information held by organisations and to challenge its accuracy and completeness.
- **CPPA, Section 55 (proposed)** — Explicit right of disposal: individuals may request deletion of personal data; organisations must comply unless retention is required by law.
- **Quebec Act respecting the protection of personal information in the private sector (Law 25, 2022)** — Explicit right to erasure and to de-indexation of personal data; applies to all organisations processing data of Quebec residents.

**Key Case:** *Google Inc. v. Equustek Solutions Inc. (2017, Supreme Court of Canada)* — Established that global de-indexing orders can be issued by Canadian courts, extending the territorial reach of privacy enforcement.

---

### 🇰🇷 South Korea — PIPA (Personal Information Protection Act)
**Act No. 10465 — enacted 2011, significantly amended 2020 & 2023**

- **Article 36** — Right to correction and deletion of personal data. Data subjects may request the deletion of their personal data; controllers must comply unless retention is required by law.
- **Article 37** — Right to suspension of processing.
- **Article 39-3** — Mandatory deletion of personal data upon termination of service or upon user withdrawal.

Enforcement: The Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) has imposed fines of up to KRW 69.2 billion ($50 million USD) against major platforms for violations.

---

### 🇦🇷 Argentina — PDPA (Personal Data Protection Act)
**Law No. 25,326 — enacted 2000, with subsequent amendments**

- **Article 16** — Right of correction, updating, and *suppression*. Data subjects have the right to have incorrect, inaccurate, or outdated data corrected or deleted without delay.
- **Article 43** — Constitutional *habeas data* action: a constitutional remedy allowing any person to demand access to, correction, or deletion of data held about them in public registers or databases.

Argentina was among the first countries in the world to constitutionalise personal data rights.

---

### 🇵🇭 Philippines — Data Privacy Act
**Republic Act No. 10173 — enacted 2012**

- **Section 16(d)** — Right to erasure or blocking. Data subjects have the right to dispute the inaccuracy or error in personal data and to have it corrected immediately, and to have it blocked, removed, or destroyed upon withdrawal of consent or upon finding that the data is incomplete, outdated, false, unlawfully obtained, or no longer necessary.

---

### 🇯🇵 Japan — APPI (Act on the Protection of Personal Information)
**Act No. 57 of 2003 — major amendment 2020, in force April 2022**

- **Article 33** — Right to disclosure: data subjects may request disclosure of retained personal data.
- **Article 34** — Right to correction, addition, and deletion of personal data.
- **Article 35** — Right to suspension of use or provision where data was obtained unlawfully or is being used beyond the stated purpose.
- **Article 24** — Data minimisation principle: personal data must not be retained beyond the period necessary to achieve the original purpose.

---

### 🇹🇷 Turkey — KVKK (Personal Data Protection Law)
**Law No. 6698 — enacted 2016, modelled on EU Directive 95/46/EC**

- **Article 7** — Deletion, destruction, and anonymisation of personal data. Controllers must delete, destroy, or anonymise personal data when the purpose of processing ceases or the legal basis no longer applies.
- **Article 11(e)** — Explicit right of data subjects to request deletion or destruction of their personal data.
- **Article 11(f)** — Right to demand that third parties to whom data has been transferred are also notified of the deletion.

---

### 🇷🇸 Serbia — Law on Personal Data Protection
**Official Gazette RS, No. 87/2018 — in force 2019, modelled on GDPR**

- **Article 30** — Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten"), mirroring GDPR Article 17 in structure and scope.
- **Article 31** — Right to restriction of processing.
- **Article 25** — Data protection by design and by default.

---

### 🇺🇸 United States — State-Level Frameworks
*The US has no federal right to erasure. Individual states legislate independently.*

- **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) / CPRA, Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.105** — Right to deletion: consumers may request deletion of personal data; businesses must comply and must instruct service providers to delete as well.
- **Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (CDPA), § 59.1-577(A)(4)** — Right to delete personal data provided by or obtained about the consumer.
- **Colorado Privacy Act (CPA), C.R.S. § 6-1-1306(1)(d)** — Right to deletion of personal data.
- **Connecticut, Texas, Oregon, Montana, Indiana** and 14 further states have enacted comparable deletion rights since 2023.

The US approach differs fundamentally: deletion rights apply only to data the organisation holds *about* the requesting consumer; there is no equivalent to the EU's de-listing right from search engines under the First Amendment.

---

### 🌐 International Human Rights Instruments

- **Article 12, Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948)** — No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with their privacy. The right to control one's personal narrative is a universally recognised dimension of this protection.

- **Article 17, International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR, 1966)** — No one shall be subjected to arbitrary or unlawful interference with their privacy, family, home, or correspondence. Ratified by 174 states.

- **Article 8, European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)** — Right to respect for private and family life. The European Court of Human Rights has directly linked this provision to the right to be forgotten in the digital context.

- **Convention 108+ (Council of Europe, modernised 2018)** — The only binding international treaty on data protection, open to non-EU states. Article 9 requires that data be deleted when it is no longer necessary for the purpose for which it was stored.
